# Channel Partner One-Pagers

Four one-pagers for secondary buyers — the people who recommend or deploy Primer at client organizations. Each is written in the language that persona actually uses. Attach the relevant one to a cold email.

## For the PE Operating Partner

**Headline:** One scorecard. Every portco. No new SaaS contract per company.

You own twelve portfolio companies, and every one of them has a different performance tool. Lattice at one, 15Five at another, spreadsheets at three, and nothing at all at the remaining handful. You've been asked to standardize, and every vendor quote you get back wants a multi-year contract per portco plus implementation fees per portco. The math doesn't work.

Primer is a one-time $5,000 license per legal entity. Each portco buys its own license, you deploy the same framework across all of them, and the operating team gets a standard five-tier scorecard they can actually compare across the portfolio. Total cost for twelve portcos is $60,000, one time, forever. No renewals. No seat fees as a portco grows. No consolidation headache when one gets sold.

The framework is what you care about. Every metric has an owner, a weight, and a tier. Every leader sees a composite score for their scope. Cross-functional disputes go through a formal Inquiry process that doesn't require you to fly to the portco to resolve it. When you swap a CEO, the incoming CEO sees exactly where the business stands on day one, in the same format as every other business in the fund.

**Next step:** Take 30 minutes to walk one portco team through the framework. If they don't get it, you haven't committed to anything. If they do, the portco buys the license that afternoon.

## For the Fractional CFO

**Headline:** Stop line-iteming a performance tool. Own it and move on.

You walk into a 75-person company and find they're paying $24,000 a year for a performance tool that three managers actually use. You recommend cancelling it, and HR pushes back because they chose it, and now the cancellation is political. Meanwhile the actual problem, which is that nobody in the company agrees on what "good" looks like for any function, is not being solved by the tool they're paying for.

Primer turns the performance-management line into a capital expense instead of an operating expense. One payment of $5,000, amortized across the useful life of the asset, hits the balance sheet the way an ERP or an accounting package does. It's the kind of purchase a CFO approves without asking anyone for permission, because it's smaller than a laptop refresh and it replaces a recurring cost.

Primer also gives you something a performance tool usually doesn't: a weighted composite score for every leader in the company. When you sit down with the CEO for the monthly review, you can point at a number and say "this is where operations is, this is where sales is, this is where engineering is, and here is what each team agreed counts." It becomes a CFO's dashboard as much as an HR tool, which is why CFOs end up being the ones who recommend it.

**Next step:** Run the cost calculator on the current performance tool. Five-year savings are almost always six figures at 100+ headcount. The number does the selling.

## For the EOS Implementer

**Headline:** A real scorecard. Your clients will actually open it.

You've been running EOS engagements for four years. Every client gets a scorecard spreadsheet in session two, promises to update it every Monday, and then the spreadsheet rots by session six. You end up walking into the Level 10 meeting to find last month's numbers, or no numbers, or numbers that only one person on the leadership team actually believes. The framework works, but the tool for running it does not.

Primer is the tool. It implements the scorecard the way EOS describes it: each leader owns their measurables, each measurable has a clear standard, and the whole leadership team sees the same numbers on the same cadence. The five tiers (Alarm, Concern, Content, Effective, Optimized) map onto the red/yellow/green language your clients already use, with more resolution so "yellow" doesn't mean three different things to three different leaders.

Because Primer ships as source code, you rebrand it once, load your own metric templates, and deploy it at every client engagement. Each client buys their own license at $5,000, one-time. When you walk out at the end of the engagement, the client owns the source and the deployment, and your work keeps running without you.

**Next step:** Load Primer on your laptop this week, spend 20 minutes adding the measurables from your next client's V/TO, and bring the running product to your next session. The conversation changes the moment they see their own numbers in the tier view.

## For the Operations Consultant

**Headline:** Leave something running when the engagement ends.

You've spent fifteen years helping companies fix their operations. Every engagement follows the same arc: you diagnose the problem, build a framework, train the team, and hand over a spreadsheet or a deck that's supposed to keep it all going. Six months later, the spreadsheet is stale, the deck is in someone's archive folder, and the client calls you back to do it again.

Primer breaks that cycle. Your client buys a $5,000 perpetual license for their legal entity, and you configure it during the engagement: their metrics, their weights, their tier definitions. The framework you built lives inside a running application their team opens every week instead of a file they forget exists. You rebrand it to match the client's identity or your own firm's, and the source code means you can add whatever custom views the engagement calls for.

When you walk out, the client owns the deployment and the source. They don't need you to keep it running, and they don't need DavidPM either. But here's what actually happens: clients who use Primer during an engagement call you back for the next engagement, not because the tool broke, but because the framework worked and they want you to extend it. You stop being the person who diagnoses problems and start being the person who built the system that solved them.

**Next step:** Pick your next engagement. Load the client's top five operational metrics into Primer on your laptop. Walk into the kickoff meeting with a running product instead of a slide deck.
