# Primer Pitch Deck — 12 Slides

Ready to send. Drop your name, photo, and contact on slide 12. The rest travels as-is.

Speaker notes sit under each slide in italic. Everything on a slide is what the buyer actually sees.

---

## Slide 1 — Title

> **Performance management you actually own.**
>
> One payment. Your infrastructure. No subscription.
>
> Primer by DavidPM

_Speaker note: The title slide does one job. It tells the buyer this conversation is not about another SaaS subscription. If they flinched the last time someone pitched them Lattice, this line tells them to keep reading._

---

## Slide 2 — The problem, in three numbers

> **95%** of managers hate their performance system.
>
> **2%** of Fortune 500 CHROs say their system inspires employees.
>
> **$35M/year** — what a 10,000-person company burns running reviews no one trusts.

_Speaker note: Stats come from /docs/leadership-pain-reference.md. Do not soften them. The room should be uncomfortable by the end of this slide. If the buyer pushes back on the 95% number, that IS the conversation — they already agree the system is broken and just want to argue about the decimal._

---

## Slide 3 — Why the SaaS tools don't fix it

Three bullets, each with one sentence underneath:

- **They're optimized for the vendor's roadmap, not yours.** You wait quarters for features their product team prioritized.
- **They charge per seat, forever.** A 200-person company pays $36K a year and owns nothing at the end of it.
- **Your employee data lives on their servers.** Every breach, every sub-processor change, every contract renegotiation is theirs to drive.

_Speaker note: Do not name Lattice, 15Five, etc. on this slide. The buyer will name them for you. If you put the logos up, it becomes a feature fight and you lose. Keep it categorical._

---

## Slide 4 — What Primer is

> **A framework, not a dashboard.**
>
> Leaders propose their own metrics. Managers approve them. Every metric gets an explicit weight. Every person sees a composite score built from those weights. Cross-functional conflicts go through a formal Inquiry process instead of hallway politics.
>
> Five tiers: Alarm, Concern, Content, Effective, Optimized. No 1-to-10 score. No pass/fail.

_Speaker note: The pitch is the framework, not the software. The software is how the framework runs. If the buyer loves the framework, the software sells itself. If they don't love the framework, no feature list saves you._

---

## Slide 5 — The cost comparison

Show the 5-year savings table for the buyer's approximate headcount. Use the calculator from the kit and drop the exact numbers in before you send.

Example for a 250-person company at $15/user/month:

| Horizon | SaaS cost | Primer cost | Savings      |
| ------- | --------- | ----------- | ------------ |
| 3 years | $135,000  | $5,000      | $130,000     |
| 5 years | $225,000  | $5,000      | **$220,000** |

_Speaker note: This is the slide that closes the deal. Do not talk while the buyer reads it. Count to ten in your head. Nine times out of ten they break the silence themselves with a question about data migration or implementation, which means you've already won the price conversation._

## Slide 6 — Data sovereignty

> **Your data never leaves your servers.**
>
> Self-hosted. Air-gapped. No telemetry. No license validation pings. Full source code, auditable line by line. Your security team owns the deployment, the patches, and the access controls.
>
> If DavidPM disappears tomorrow, your Primer deployment keeps running. Forever.

_Speaker note: For a CIO this is the whole pitch. For an HR director it's a nice-to-have. Read the room and either spend 90 seconds here or 15._

---

## Slide 7 — The low-risk experiment

> **$5,000. One legal entity. No renewal.**
>
> If it works, you keep it forever. If it doesn't, you spent less than one month of a Lattice contract.
>
> There is no version of this where $5,000 is the expensive option.

_Speaker note: Frame the purchase as a test, not a commitment. Buyers who cannot get a $50K SaaS budget approved can get $5K approved out of petty cash. This is the slide where they stop asking "should we do this?" and start asking "whose credit card do we use?"._

## Slide 10 — The objection you're about to raise

> **"But we're not a dev shop. We can't maintain source code."**
>
> You don't have to. Primer works out of the box. Deploy it with `docker compose up`, create an admin account, and you're running. Updates are optional and manual. Most customers never touch the code; they just own it.
>
> Owning the code is insurance, not homework.

_Speaker note: Put this slide in the deck whether or not the buyer has said the line yet. Every buyer is thinking it. Answering it before they say it out loud makes you look like you've done this before._

---

## Slide 11 — How the next 30 days look

1. **Today:** 30-minute technical demo with your IT lead in the room.
2. **Week 1:** Purchase the license. Receive the zip.
3. **Week 2:** Deploy on a staging server. Run it through your security review.
4. **Week 3:** Configure metrics for one pilot team.
5. **Week 4:** Go live with the pilot. Roll out to the rest of the company on your timeline, not ours.

_Speaker note: Always give a concrete 30-day path. Abstract pitches lose. A buyer who can picture week 2 is a buyer who can get internal approval._
