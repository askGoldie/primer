## 1. Performance Management Is Universally Broken

The system designed to improve performance actively damages it.

95% of managers express frustration with their company's review system. Only 14% of employees strongly agree their performance reviews motivate improvement. Traditional reviews make performance worse one-third of the time. 85% of employees would consider quitting after receiving what they perceive as an unfair performance review. 22% of employees have called in sick rather than face a performance review, 35% have complained to peers, and 15% have cried.

The people responsible for running these systems know they are failing. Only 2% of CHROs from Fortune 500 companies strongly agree that their performance management system inspires employees to improve. 77% of HR directors acknowledge their evaluations fail to accurately measure employee contributions.

The cost is measurable. A company with 10,000 employees spends roughly $35 million per year conducting reviews. Managers spend an average of 210 hours per year on performance management activities. 61% of senior managers say at least half the time spent on decision-making is ineffective.

## 2. Middle Management Feels Invisible, Powerless, and Squeezed

82% of middle managers report feeling invisible and frustrated. They describe themselves as burned out and powerless. They are squeezed from both directions: senior leadership imposes targets from above while their teams demand more coaching, flexibility, and development from below.

37% of managers report increased pressure from the teams they lead. 39% say pressure from senior leadership has increased year over year. 34% of employees say they never want a management role. One in four managers admit they are completely miserable at work.

Organizations claim they empower mid-level leaders. The leaders disagree. Research shows that permission alone is not sufficient to create empowerment. Many executive leaders are not letting go of authority or vesting their power to middle managers. The result is frustration at the top, where permission has been granted, and frustration in the middle, where nothing has actually changed.

76% of organizations have significantly updated their leadership programs and plan to increase spending, yet 71% of HR leaders admit they are not effectively developing their mid-level leaders. 96% of experienced leaders report being desperate for targeted, experience-appropriate training. 70% of managers have received no training on how to lead hybrid teams. 84% of workers say poorly trained managers create unnecessary work and stress.

VPs and directors experience a specific version of this pain. They spend their time translating between two groups of people operating in parallel universes. They are citizens of both the executive world and the operational world while not fully belonging to either. They receive strategic goals they did not define and must translate them into operational metrics for teams who did not choose them.

## 3. Goals Are Imposed, Not Owned

Most organizations measure leadership through top-down KPIs that leaders had no role in defining. This produces compliance, not engagement. Leaders hit numbers without internalizing what those numbers mean.

When KPIs are imposed top-down without involving the people expected to meet them, the result is widespread dissatisfaction, low morale, high turnover, and feedback indicating that employees feel disconnected from the company's objectives. When employees are actively involved in their own goal setting, they are two times as likely to have clear expectations. Organizations that involve employees in goal-setting witness a 12% increase in productivity.

Only about 50% of U.S. employees strongly agree they know what is expected of them at work, down from 56% in early 2020. When people do not know what "good" looks like, they do not execute confidently, and performance management becomes reactive.

Managers account for roughly 70% of the variance in team engagement. The quality of goal-setting, feedback, coaching, and clarity from managers is the primary driver of whether strategy turns into execution. If managers are not trained, goal-setting becomes guesswork. And guesswork becomes disengagement.

Employee engagement fell to 21% globally in 2024. The estimated cost of that decline: $438 billion in lost productivity.

## 4. Cross-Functional Metrics Create Invisible Damage

Each department creates its own dashboard. This produces conflicting truths. Sales says they hit targets, but fulfillment is struggling. Marketing reports rising engagement, but customer support sees rising complaints. With no cross-functional visibility, each department makes decisions in silos, creating friction, inefficiencies, and strategic blind spots.

When KPIs do not reflect real effort or outcomes, teams feel frustrated. Reps might hit targets that do not contribute to profit. Operations might meet speed goals at the cost of quality. The dependency between one leader's metric and another leader's metric remains invisible until it causes damage.

Cross-functional concerns that span organizational silos have no structured mechanism for resolution in most companies. Leaders who believe another team's misaligned metric is affecting their own operation have no formal channel to surface that concern. The problem escalates informally through complaints, politics, or crisis.

Communication gaps fragment shared metrics across departments, creating incomplete performance pictures. Departments managing interdependent metrics often operate in isolation. Cross-functional teams struggle to align efforts without current performance data, leading to duplicated work and conflicting priorities.

## 5. Metrics Go Stale and No One Has a Process to Fix Them

KPIs that are not regularly reviewed become outdated and lose their relevance. Organizations continue to focus on metrics that no longer reflect business strategy, market conditions, or operational reality.

Metrics that remain static for months without meaningful change should raise a red flag. If a KPI has not been updated to reflect new business priorities, it is no longer serving its purpose. Sometimes KPIs linger simply because someone thought it was a good idea to track them, but no one can remember why.

An excessive focus on meeting specific KPI metrics stifles innovation. Employees and managers become less likely to pursue novel ideas or creative solutions that do not directly contribute to the metrics being tracked. This prevents companies from adapting to new market demands or exploring growth opportunities.

Over-reliance on dashboard snapshots creates a false sense of security. Managers assume that if primary KPIs are being met, all aspects of the business are performing well, overlooking underlying issues not captured by the current metrics. This is especially common when KPIs are not regularly updated to reflect the changing business landscape.

There is no structured, recurring process for leaders to challenge whether their metrics still make sense. When metrics become irrelevant due to changing conditions, there is no formal mechanism for updating them. The result is rigidity followed by crisis-driven transformation.

## 6. Everything Is Equally Important, So Nothing Is Prioritized

Leaders operate under dozens of metrics treated with equal urgency. Without a mechanism for explicit weighting, leaders and their teams remain constantly reactive. There is no structured conversation that forces a leader to say which metric matters most.

The best practice recommendation is to track between four and ten KPIs per area of business. In practice, organizations track far more. Too many KPIs dilute focus and work against data-driven decisions. The risk is that data becomes convoluted, defeating the entire purpose of measurement.

When leaders have not explicitly stated "safety is 40% and schedule is 30% and budget is 30%," they have not made a strategic statement. Without that statement, misalignment between a leader's actual priorities and their organization's expectations remains hidden and non-negotiable.

## 7. Intrinsic Motivation Is Proven but Structurally Absent

Self-determination theory is one of the most replicated findings in organizational psychology. Three psychological needs must be fulfilled to adequately motivate workers: competence (mastery of their environment), autonomy (agency over their own behavior rather than feeling controlled by external pressures), and relatedness (meaningful connections with other people). Meta-analytic evidence shows that satisfying these three needs is associated with better performance, reduced burnout, more organizational commitment, and reduced turnover.

When individuals understand the worth and purpose of their jobs, feel ownership and autonomy in carrying them out, and receive clear feedback and supports, they are likely to become more autonomously motivated and reliably perform better, learn better, and be better adjusted. When motivation is controlled through contingent rewards or power dynamics, the extrinsic focus that results narrows the range of employees' efforts and produces short-term compliance rather than sustained engagement.

Autonomy-supportive behaviors include providing employees with choice in task engagement, giving a rationale for assigned work-related tasks, and allowing employees to voice their opinions and have a say in how tasks are performed. Interventions where supervisors display behaviors that promote greater choice, autonomous reasons, and responsibility and ownership lead to greater employee engagement and satisfaction.

Managers who offer autonomy support, which means helping employees make progress by giving meaningful feedback, choice over how to do things, and encouragement, produce higher job satisfaction and better job performance.

Despite universal agreement on these principles, most organizations still default to extrinsic systems. The standard performance management stack (imposed KPIs, annual reviews, forced ranking) structurally violates all three psychological needs. Leaders do not participate in defining what success means (no autonomy). There is no visible progression system showing operational growth (no mastery). There is no mechanism connecting one leader's performance to the health of the broader organization (no purpose).

## 8. The Measurement Tyranny Problem

Measurement should inform judgment, not replace it. In The Tyranny of Metrics, Professor Jerry Muller examines the belief that success requires quantifying everything, then rewarding or punishing based on the numbers. Many organizations fixate on dashboards, charts, and spreadsheets instead of performance.

When KPIs become the criteria used to reward and punish, problems arise. Targets can be subjective and influenced by biases or assumptions of the individual or team responsible for setting them. This leads to unrealistic or unattainable targets, or targets that fail to account for important factors that impact business performance.

When employees perceive KPI targets as unattainable, they become demotivated or frustrated, leading to a decline in productivity, morale, and job satisfaction. Unattainable targets can also lead to gaming: employees optimizing for the metric rather than the outcome the metric was supposed to represent.

The research on rewards and pay within self-determination theory has consistently shown that whether rewards have a positive, neutral, or negative effect on intrinsic motivation depends on the interpersonal context in which they are administered. Rewards administered in autonomy-supportive contexts have different effects than rewards administered in controlling contexts. The same metric can motivate or demotivate depending on whether the person being measured participated in defining it.

## 9. The VP and Director Experience: Both Ends of the Dysfunction

VPs are paid to make the plan. Directors are paid to drive results with little supervision. Both are evaluated on goals they may not have defined, using metrics they may not have chosen, against thresholds that may not reflect their operational reality.

A VP translates executive strategy into departmental execution. A director translates departmental strategy into team-level action. At each translation layer, context is lost and misalignment compounds. The VP of Marketing has dashboards showing rising MQLs. The VP of Sales has reports showing stagnant conversions. The VP of Customer Success sees declining renewal rates. Each dashboard tells a true story. No dashboard tells the whole story.

Directors focus on managing specific teams or functions within a department. They operate at a more tactical level compared to VPs but still hold strategic responsibility. They are directly accountable for execution against metrics that were often defined at a level above them.

The devaluation of management roles has become a documented trend. The "conscious unbossing" movement reflects growing reluctance among younger workers to take on management roles. Organizations that underestimate the importance of middle management risk a leadership pipeline crisis. Global HR analyst Josh Bersin has stated he has never seen such low levels of investment and maturity in leadership development in 25 years.
