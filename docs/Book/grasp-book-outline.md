# Book Outline: Working Title TBD

## Intrinsic motivation applied to operational leadership

### Primer by DavidPM | primer.company

---

## Format and Scope

Target length: 15,000 to 20,000 words (roughly 80 to 100 pages in trade paperback). A CEO finishes this on a cross-country flight or in a focused evening. The book is designed to be read multiple times. First read builds understanding. Second read maps concepts to the reader's own organization. Third read becomes a reference for specific conversations.

Tone: Direct, confident, no filler. Every paragraph earns its space.

Price point: $15 to $20 (print), $9.99 (digital). The book is a door.

Design principle: Repetition over revelation. The core ideas (autonomy in thresholds, mastery in tiers, purpose in inquiry) appear in every chapter from different angles. A reader who picks the book up six months later and opens to any chapter hits the same principles applied to a different context. The framework is small enough to hold in your head. The book's job is to make it stick.

## Structure: Seven Chapters

### Chapter 1: The Problem With How We Measure Leaders

_2,000 to 2,500 words_

The case for change. Opens with the universal experience: metrics are green, the operation does not feel healthy, and the CEO cannot explain the gap. Moves quickly through the core problems: metrics imposed from the outside produce compliance, not ownership. Leaders hit numbers without internalizing what the numbers mean. When conditions change, nobody has a mechanism for challenging or updating what gets measured. The CEO gets filtered truth because reporting structures naturally compress information into narratives.

Introduces intrinsic motivation as the missing design principle. Covers the research concisely: Deci and Ryan's Self-Determination Theory (autonomy, competence, relatedness), the key findings on external rewards undermining performance on complex tasks, and Pink's adaptation (autonomy, mastery, purpose). Does not linger. Establishes that leadership is complex cognitive work and that systems designed around extrinsic pressure produce diminishing returns.

Closes with the three needs that any measurement system must serve if it wants engaged leaders rather than compliant ones: the need to participate in defining success (autonomy), the need to see a progression (mastery), and the need to connect individual work to something larger (purpose).

This chapter is the argument. Everything after it is the answer.

### Chapter 2: Five Tiers

_2,000 to 2,500 words_

Introduces the five states of operational health: Alarm, Concern, Content, Effective, Optimized. Each tier is a state with an implied action, not a grade. Two leaders can both be Optimized. Two can both be at Alarm. The framework measures operational state, not relative standing.

Introduces threshold calibration: each leader defines what each tier looks like for each of their metrics. This is where autonomy lives. The CEO assigns the metric. The leader defines the meaning of each state. A deployment frequency of fewer than two per month might be Alarm for one engineering team and Content for another. The tiers are universal. The calibration is personal.

Introduces the threshold conversation as the primary value driver: how to have it (start with extremes, fill in the middle), what good calibration looks like (specific, differentiated, honest about Optimized), and what the CEO should watch for (sandbagging, anchoring, copying peers).

The mastery connection: the five tiers create a visible gradient from reactive crisis management to proactive optimization. Leaders can see where they are and what growth looks like. Binary systems (hit target / missed target) offer no sense of movement. Five tiers do.

Cross-industry example: show the same five tiers applied to two different functions in two different industries to demonstrate that the language is universal while the calibration is specific.

### Chapter 3: The Weight of What Matters

_2,000 to 2,500 words_

Introduces weighting: each leader assigns weights to their metrics, summing to 100%. The CEO reviews and can override. When a leader says "safety is 40%, schedule is 30%, budget is 30%," they have made an explicit strategic statement. When those weights do not match the CEO's priorities, the misalignment becomes visible and negotiable rather than hidden.

Introduces the composite score briefly: tier values (1 through 5) multiplied by weights, summed. A single number that reflects weighted operational health. The math is simple. The conversation that produces the inputs is where the value lives.

The case for restraint: three to seven metrics. Why fewer metrics produce stronger signals, more meaningful weights, and deeper threshold conversations. Why fifteen metrics creates the same noise the framework is designed to replace.

The autonomy connection reinforced: leaders do not choose whether they have metrics, but they participate in defining thresholds and proposing weights. This is structured participation, not unlimited freedom. The CEO retains authority. The leader gains ownership.

### Chapter 4: The Right to Challenge

_2,000 to 2,500 words_

Introduces the inquiry process. Two types: self-inquiry (a leader challenges their own metric, on cycle) and peer inquiry (a leader challenges a peer's metric, at any time, but must show an affected metric in their own stack).

Every inquiry requires three elements: the target metric being challenged, the affected metric in the challenger's stack, and the rationale. This structure prevents political challenges. You must demonstrate skin in the game.

Routing and transparency: both parties see everything. The authority (one level above both) decides whether evaluation is warranted and serves as tiebreaker.

The purpose connection: when a leader files an inquiry showing how their metric depends on a peer's metric, they are making organizational dependencies visible. Individual performance connects to organizational health through the structure of the process. Purpose is not a mission statement. It is an architecture.

Why inquiry is a feature, not a failure: the ability to challenge means the framework adapts. Previous systems died because they could not evolve. Inquiry normalizes continuous change as a rhythm, not an event.

The fractal architecture, briefly: the framework is identical at every level of the organization. Each leader operates as the CEO-equivalent for their silo. It only needs to be learned once. It scales when the organization scales.

### Chapter 5: The First Conversation

_2,500 to 3,000 words_

How to introduce the framework without triggering defensiveness, consultant fatigue, or the assumption that the CEO is running an audit.

The recommended approach: do not announce a framework. Ask a question. "What are the three to five things that matter most in your area? Not your KPIs. The things that would keep you up at night if they went badly."

Walk through the first one-on-one: ask for extremes, then weights. The framework is operating before the leader knows they are using a framework. Introduce the five-tier vocabulary after every senior leader has completed the conversation. Formalize what they already described.

Handle the common objections: "We already have KPIs." "This is just another framework." "I don't need more measurement." "This feels like micromanagement." Each objection gets a concise, direct response.

Where the framework fits with what the reader already uses: OKRs set goals, the quintile framework measures health. EOS provides meeting structure, the quintile framework provides leadership assessment. Lean optimizes processes, the quintile framework measures the leaders managing those processes. Balanced Scorecard organizes perspectives, the quintile framework adds weighting and threshold calibration. Brief treatment. The point is coexistence, not competition.

The single-operator version: for CEOs without management layers, the framework works as a personal operating tool. Define your own metrics, calibrate your own thresholds, weight your own priorities, track your own composite score over time. When you hire your first manager, the framework scales naturally.

This is the most practical chapter. A CEO should be able to close the book after this chapter and start the first conversation tomorrow.

### Chapter 6: What Good Looks Like

_2,000 to 2,500 words_

The quality chapter. How to tell whether the framework is working and how to recognize the common failure modes.

Good thresholds: specific, differentiated, honest about Optimized, grounded in observable criteria. Bad thresholds: vague, clustered in the middle, sandbagged, copied from peers.

Good metric selection: coverage across operational dimensions, independence between metrics, actionable by the leader, measurable with reasonable objectivity. The leading vs. lagging indicator mix: functions with long feedback loops lean toward leading indicators, functions with short loops can lean toward lagging.

Signs the framework is working: the threshold conversations are producing genuine strategic dialogue, the weighting exercise is revealing priority misalignments the CEO did not know existed, the inquiry process is being used (not avoided), and leaders are engaging with the system as a diagnostic tool rather than treating it as a compliance exercise.

Signs the framework is failing: thresholds that never change, weights that all cluster around equal, inquiries that never get filed, composite scores that never move. These are symptoms of mechanical adoption without genuine engagement. The fix is almost always to go back to the threshold conversation and have it again with more depth.

The three intrinsic motivation principles revisited one more time: if leaders are not engaged, check autonomy (are they genuinely participating in threshold definition?), check mastery (can they see progression across the tiers?), check purpose (does the inquiry process connect their work to organizational health?). The diagnosis maps to the design.

### Chapter 7: A Common Language

_1,500 to 2,000 words_

The closing chapter. Short, forward-looking.

The translation problem: CEOs spend most of their peer time translating operational context. A construction CEO and a healthcare CEO have more in common than they realize, but no shared vocabulary to discover it. "My VP of Operations is at Alarm on two metrics and just filed a peer inquiry against Sales" communicates instantly to another CEO running the same framework, regardless of industry.

The language travels with the people who use it. Every leader inside an adopting organization who learns to think in five tiers carries that vocabulary when they move to their next role. Every CEO who uses the framework brings it into the peer settings they already inhabit: Vistage, YPO, board conversations, informal CEO dinners. The framework does not require a dedicated network to spread. It spreads through use.

What happens as the vocabulary becomes common: peer conversations become more efficient. Board reporting becomes more precise. Leadership assessments become more honest. Organizations that share the language can exchange operational insight without spending half the conversation on context-setting.

The invitation: primer.company is where the framework lives, and where the source code is available for organizations that want to deploy it at scale. Mentioned once, concisely, without a sales pitch. The book has already done the selling by being useful.

## Design for Rereading

The three intrinsic motivation principles (autonomy, mastery, purpose) are not confined to Chapter 1. They surface in every chapter:

- Chapter 2 connects autonomy to thresholds and mastery to the five-tier gradient
- Chapter 3 reinforces autonomy through weighting
- Chapter 4 connects purpose to the inquiry process
- Chapter 5 shows how the first conversation activates all three
- Chapter 6 uses all three as a diagnostic for framework health
- Chapter 7 connects purpose to the broader vision of a common language

On first read, this creates a coherent argument. On second read, the repetition builds fluency. On third read, the reader is not learning the principles. They are recognizing them in their own organization.
